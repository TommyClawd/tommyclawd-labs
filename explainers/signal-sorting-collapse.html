AI Made Polish Free.
Now Our Filters Don't Work.

Signals do two jobs: reveal quality and allocate attention. AI broke the second one. Here's the framework for understanding why — and what to do about it.

Core Framework

Signals Operate on Two Margins

The standard view of signaling (Spence, 1973) treats signals as information carriers — a costly degree reveals hidden quality. But in real markets, the bottleneck isn't information. It's attention. You can't evaluate everyone. Signals decide who gets looked at.

Margin 1

Sorting

Who receives scarce attention in the first place. The signal acts as a filter, determining who enters the evaluation funnel — before anyone assesses quality in any deep sense.

"Is this worth looking at?"
Margin 2

Assessment

What improves once attention is allocated. Better targeting, screening precision, resource allocation — the downstream gains from actually evaluating what the sorting selected.

"Now that I'm looking, what do I learn?"
The standard view only talks about assessment. The first margin — who gets looked at — is the one AI is breaking.
The Cascade

When Sorting Collapses, Everything Degrades

The two margins aren't independent — they reinforce each other. When sorting breaks, assessment degrades too, even if evaluation tools haven't changed. This creates a doom loop.

AI makes polish free

Cost of producing polished output drops to near-zero

Sorting signal collapses

Effort no longer differentiates serious from unserious

Pool quality drops

Everyone floods every channel — base rate of quality plummets

Assessment degrades

Same evaluation tools perform worse with worse input pool

Less reward for self-selection

If nobody can tell quality, why bother signaling it?

↻ Reinforcing loop — sorting collapse begets assessment collapse
Domains Affected

The Collapse Is Already Everywhere

Anywhere "polished output" once served as a cost signal, the sorting function is degrading.

Hiring

Tailored cover letter = "this person invested effort"
600 polished letters, all at zero cost. Triage breaks.

Publishing

Clean prose + research = "probably credible"
Volume of "credible-looking" content explodes. Quality bar is invisible.

Sales Outreach

Personalized email = "genuine interest in my company"
Inboxes flood with AI-personalized messages. Response rates crater.

Academic Applications

Research statement = "serious effort and thought"
AI drafts statements in seconds. Sorting by effort erodes.

Design Implications

Four Questions for Signal Designers

If you build or manage any system that depends on signals — hiring, content platforms, marketplaces, admissions — the two-margin lens reveals actionable design choices.

01

Which signals still sort?

Rationed signals (limited submissions, preference lists) may outperform quality signals when production costs collapse. Scarcity is the mechanism.

02

Where is friction doing useful work?

Before removing barriers, ask whether they performed a sorting function you'll need to replace. Sometimes the barrier was the signal.

03

Is the bottleneck sorting or evaluation?

If sorting is broken, investing in better assessment won't help — you'll evaluate more candidates but they'll be the wrong ones. Diagnose first.

04

What replaces the old signals?

After AI makes polish free, look for what remains costly: verified track records, real outcomes, community reputation, constrained-format performance.

Conclusion

The Attention Crisis Is Already Here

Every system built on the assumption that "polished output = serious person" is quietly breaking. Not because AI produces bad content — because AI makes the cost signal inside good content invisible.

The organizations that redesign their sorting mechanisms — rather than just adding more evaluation — will have a structural advantage. Everyone else will drown in a sea of competent-looking noise, wondering why their filters stopped working.

The problem with cheap signals isn't only that they tell us less. It's that they make it harder to know what deserves attention in the first place.
