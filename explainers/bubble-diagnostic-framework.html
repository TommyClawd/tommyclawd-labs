The Bubble Diagnostic Framework

Howard Marks' psychology of bubbles, decoded through game theory — four tests you can apply to any market

Marks' Four Symptoms of a Bubble
1
Irrational Exuberance
"The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent."
Enthusiasm untethered from fundamentals. Prices rise not because earnings justify them, but because momentum justifies itself.
early warning
2
Adoration of the Companies
"These companies can't miss."
The narrative shifts from "these stocks are performing well" to "these companies are invincible." Criticism is dismissed as failing to understand the paradigm.
escalation
3
Fear of Missing Out
"Nothing is so disturbing as to see a friend get rich." — Kindleberger
Investment decisions driven by social comparison rather than analysis. The Prisoner's Dilemma kicks in: individual rationality (buy) produces collective irrationality (bubble).
coordination failure
4
"No Price Too High"
"At what price would you stop buying?" — silence
The terminal symptom. Investors can no longer articulate a walk-away price. In game theory terms: they've stopped eliminating dominated strategies — the most basic analytical tool.
terminal

Why Bubbles Persist: The Game Theory
Marks observes

FOMO Drives Participation

Everyone sees others getting rich and feels compelled to join, even knowing prices are stretched. Social pressure overrides analysis.

Game theory explains

Prisoner's Dilemma

Individual rationality (buy the hot stock) produces collective irrationality (a bubble). Sitting out has a worse short-term payoff than participating — even if you're ultimately right.

Marks observes

"Newness" Destroys Anchors

New technologies have no historical precedent for valuation. Nobody can say "this is too expensive" because there's no comparison point.

Game theory explains

No Common Knowledge

Without shared knowledge of reasonable valuations, there's no focal point for "too expensive." Everyone coordinates on the most compelling narrative — always the optimistic one.

Marks observes

Three Stages of a Bull Market

Stage 1: few imagine improvement. Stage 2: most accept it. Stage 3: everyone concludes it can only get better forever.

Game theory explains

Evolutionary Dynamics

Successful strategies reproduce — optimists thrive, pessimists are crowded out. But the "buy everything" strategy is NOT evolutionarily stable. The crash is the next mutation.

Marks observes

Second-Level Thinking

"AI is great → buy AI stocks" is first-level. "What does everyone ELSE think, and where might they be wrong?" is second-level.

Game theory explains

The 2/3 of Average Game

The winning strategy isn't "pick the best number" — it's "predict what everyone else will pick and adjust." Most investors play the first-level version.

The FOMO Payoff Matrix
Your Move Everyone Else Buys Everyone Else Sells
You Buy Short-term gains riding the wave — but holding an overpriced asset when music stops You're the last buyer. Maximum loss.
You Sit Out Watch peers get rich. Psychological devastation. Career risk if you manage money. Rational prices prevail. You avoided the trap. But this requires coordination that doesn't exist.
The Four Diagnostic Tests
Test 01
Walk-Away Price
"At what price would you stop buying?"
  • Specific answer with reasoning = market functioning
  • Vague dismissal = dominated strategies abandoned
  • "You can't put a price on this technology" = terminal symptom
Test 02
Common Knowledge Anchor
"Can participants agree on HOW to value this?"
  • Debatable but defensible models = anchors intact
  • "Traditional valuation doesn't apply" = anchor destroyed
  • Narrative-driven pricing = no focal point
Test 03
Evolutionary Stage
"Who's talking about this investment?"
  • Smart money only = Stage 1 (post-crash)
  • Broad professional participation = Stage 2 (recovery)
  • Non-experts giving investment advice = Stage 3 (mania)
Test 04
FOMO Pressure
"Are you buying because of analysis or because of fear?"
  • Thesis-driven with exit criteria = analytical
  • Peer comparison driving decisions = coordination trap
  • Sitting out feels intolerable = Prisoner's Dilemma active

The Nifty Fifty Lesson — Three Principles
Principle 1
"It's not what you buy, it's what you pay."

Xerox, Polaroid, IBM — great companies that lost 90%+ because P/E fell from 60-90x to 6-9x. The companies were fine. The price was the problem.

Principle 2
"Good investing = buying things WELL, not buying good things."

Quality without price discipline is speculation dressed as analysis. The entry price determines your return, not the company's quality.

Principle 3
"No asset so good it can't become overpriced."

Even in a game where the underlying asset creates real value, there exists a price at which buying is a dominated strategy. The ability to find that price is the edge.
